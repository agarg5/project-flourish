# 01 — Vision & Design Pillars

## The Heart of It

Most sustainability narratives are about *harm reduction* — doing less damage,
shrinking footprints, feeling guilty. This game rejects that framing. Its thesis
(drawn from the source article) is that **humanity can become a keystone
species**: a force that makes the planet *more* alive, not less. Like elephants
carving trails and dispersing seeds, or beavers engineering wetlands, humans can
have a footprint that is *good for the environment*.

The game lets players *feel* that future by building it.

## Emotional Arc

The seven ages walk the player through the exact journey of the article:

1. **Embedded** — In the Stone Age you are just another animal in the web of life.
2. **Capable** — You gain power, settlements, technology, and impact.
3. **Tested** — The Industrial Age makes large-scale impact possible (but it is
   *not* a forced punishment — see pillars).
4. **Steward** — You gain the tools to heal and then to *improve* the planet.
5. **Flourishing** — You push toward an idealized world richer in life than the
   one you started with.

The player should finish a session feeling **hopeful and capable**, not lectured.

## Design Pillars (Use These To Resolve Disputes)

### Pillar 1 — Hope over guilt
No mechanic should shame the player. There is no "dirty path" to fall down.
Choices are between good and even-better. Pressure is gentle and self-correcting,
never punishing. If a feature makes the player feel bad, redesign it.

### Pillar 2 — Synergy, not tradeoff
Well-being and biodiversity are *not* opposed. The scoring math
(`wellbeing × biodiversity`) makes this literal: you cannot win by sacrificing
one for the other. This is the single most important mechanical expression of
the thesis.

### Pillar 3 — Embedded, not god-handed
The player is *part of* the ecosystem, not an external manager dropping animals
like inventory. Default interaction is "shape the habitat; life responds."
Reintroduction exists but is rare and earned. Every UI/UX choice should
reinforce belonging *within* the living world.

### Pillar 4 — Credible, not preachy
Buildings, technologies, animals, and restoration actions are grounded in real
science (restoration ecology, wildlife-inclusive urban design, the Hestia
superhabitability thought experiment). Realism is what makes the vision
*inspiring* rather than fantasy. When inventing content, prefer the real thing.

### Pillar 5 — Relaxed and legible
This is a calm sandbox, not a stressful optimizer. No clock in v1. Cause and
effect should be *legible* — when the player does something, they can see the
living world and the Flourishing score respond in an understandable way (very
Roller Coaster Tycoon: nurture a meter, watch it react).

### Pillar 6 — Shareable
Web-first because the point is for people to *see* this vision. A link someone
can click and play beats a polished download nobody opens. Keep load light
enough to share.

## Anti-Goals (What This Is NOT)

- Not a doom/collapse simulator.
- Not a guilt or "gotcha" climate lecture.
- Not a min-max spreadsheet optimizer (though depth is welcome under the hood).
- Not a god game where you place animals like decorations.
- Not a win/lose competitive game. It is endless and personal.

## North Star

The idealized late-game planet is inspired by **Hestia**, a hypothetical
"superhabitable" world richer in life than Earth (see file 07). The player's
horizon is to push their world toward that ceiling — eliminating dead zones,
maximizing productive habitat, and supporting biodiversity far beyond the
starting state. The final emotional beat: *humanity made the planet better.*
